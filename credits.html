If you have not played it yet, I encourage you to try doing so before reading on, and especially before looking at the mathematical source material mentioned. Asset credits and the licence can be found at the very end.

This game is an implementation of a celebrated construction by John Conway, later built upon by Noam Elkies and Jeremy Martin, generalising the famous Mathieu group M12. The basic idea is a reworking of the famous "fifteen puzzle" to have long served as an accessible introduction to the concepts of permutation parity and the alternating groups, replacing the rows and columns of the original puzzle with the lines of the projective plane PG(2, 3) over the 3-field. As in the original puzzle, all but one of the thirteen points of the projective plane are equipped with counters, and the player proceeds by moving the "hole" on the remaining point across the board in prescribed ways. Whereas the original puzzle transposes the hole with the counter of an adjacent square, however, in this game the hole is swapped with a counter on any line shared with the hole's current location (meaning, of course, any line whatsoever, since any two points determine a line), which simultaneously swaps the other two counters in that line with one another. Thus, any individual move consists of two non-overlapping transpositions of counters, one including the "hole."

While perhaps appearing rather arbitrary at first sight, the game is in fact rich with mathematical significance. Restricting ourselves to those permutations generated by sequences in which the hole is returned to its original position, the space of possible states of the board is isomorphic in the natural way to the Mathieu group M12, an object of considerable interest to algebraists. The group is, among other things, the unique sharply 5-transitive group besides the appropriate symmetric alternating groups (and one of only two 5-transitive such groups, the other being its "big brother" supergroup M24). It is simple, and indeed was among the first sporadic simple groups discovered, along with the other Mathieu groups. And it possesses, like S6, a unique outer automorphism. Before first presenting the current game, Conway had already done pioneering work in the theory of M12, including the construction of the "Kitten" as a handy device for calculating combinatoric properties of the underlying object symmetrically transformed by M12.

Considered as a presentation of M12, the Conway puzzle is illuminating in a number of ways. So far, I have been most impressed by the light it sheds on the aforementioned outer automorphism. To understand this connection, we begin by considering a small extension of the game. In addition to counters on the points of the space, we add counters to all but one of the lines, with the restriction that the "open" point be contained in the "open" line. Sequences of moves in the new game consist of alternating transpositions of point-counters and transpositions of line-counters, along with natural compatibility restrictions on adjacent point moves and line moves in the sequence. The sequences in this new game also turn out, modulo equivalence of induced permutation, to be isomorphic to M12, with the action of such a sequence on the lines being an inequivalent action of M12 on a twelve-set (appearing, in M12's guise as a stabiliser of subgroup in M24 of a certain kind of twelve-set in the 24-element Witt design of which M24 is the symmetry group, as the action of M12 on the complementary twelve-set). Given the duality of point and line in the incidence geometry of the projective plane, this gives rise to an extremely natural definition of the automorphism: it is that given by switching out the swaps of points for those of their dual lines in the sequence, and vice versa. To me, at least, this makes the outer automorphism come across as far more natural and "inevitable" than the famous S6 outer automorphism.

Most exciting, however, is the way the construction can be extended to an entirely new object, Conway's M13 pseudogroup, which consists of all permutations on the thirteen counters (including the hole) that can be induced by valid sequences of moves in the original game, with the hole starting in a designated spot. (Categorically, one can represent the space of valid moves as the "groupoid" whose objects are the possible positions of the hole, making the pseudogroup the co-slice category, also a groupoid, of arrows out of some specified such object.) Not being itself a group, n-transitivity splits into two different concepts: a set of n points eing a "universal donor" (there existing a move sequence taking their to counters to an arbitrary n-set) and its being a "universal recipient" (there existing a move sequence taking an arbitrary n-set of counters to it). In the case of n = 6, these turn out to be equivalent to, respectively, the properties of containing the initial position of the hole and of containing a line in the projective space. This provides a restricted (and quite elegant) generalisation of the transitivity of the "lower" Mathieu groups to the new pseudogroup.

The game, as you by now should be able to tell, implements this puzzle using the Ralts line as the "hole" counter, with teleportation inducing the double transpositions. The increasing numbers of lost child Pokémon are designed to illustrate the transitivity properties of the (pseudo)group, and the final level reresents the double action of M12 mentioned earlier through teleportation's effect of the "roaming legendaries" and other Pokémon as they traverse the route. I have only played a bit of the game, in the course of testing it, but already I think it's helping me better appreciate M12 (and M13). I suspect, for one, it is giving me an intuitive sense of why M12 is sharply 5-transitive, based on the natural strategy of "storing" Pokémon in "vacant" lines (though I need to finish crystallising this apparent insight into an actual proof).

The Pokémon in each level are "doubly" scrambled, each load freshly permuting both the points of the plane over the positions on the page and the counters (Pokémon) over the points. The intent is to make each playthrough of each level an exercise in both identifying and manipulating the line structure of the space, to better enable replayability.

I hope you sot something out of this game! Thanks for helping out those fretting Pokémoms, I am sure they appreciated it :)
